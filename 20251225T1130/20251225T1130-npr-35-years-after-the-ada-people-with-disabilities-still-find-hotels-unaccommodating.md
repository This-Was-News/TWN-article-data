## Article summary
Thirty-five years after the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was enacted, people with disabilities continue to face significant challenges with hotel accessibility. Despite federal laws mandating accessibility in hotels built or substantially renovated after January 26, 1993, travelers who use wheelchairs and other mobility devices report frequent issues. These problems include inaccessible rooms even when reserved, design flaws in bathrooms and showers, beds that are too high or too low for safe transfers, and a lack of accessible amenities like pool lifts and airport shuttles. The NPR survey and interviews revealed that these barriers lead to frustration, feelings of being treated as "second-class citizens," and can even deter people from traveling. Reasons for non-compliance range from the hotel industry's hesitancy to invest in upgrades, complex ownership structures leading to confusion over responsibility, inconsistent regulatory enforcement, and high staff turnover. While the hotel industry states a commitment to improving accessibility, the experiences of travelers indicate a persistent gap between legal requirements and on-the-ground reality.
